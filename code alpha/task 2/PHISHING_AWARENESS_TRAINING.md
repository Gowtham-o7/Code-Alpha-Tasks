## 1. What Is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of social engineering attack where criminals impersonate a trusted person, brand, or system to trick you into revealing sensitive information (passwords, card numbers, ID data) or into taking an action that compromises security (clicking a malicious link, opening an infected attachment, approving a fraudulent payment).

Phishing remains the number one entry point for data breaches and ransomware because it targets **people**, not technical systems. Firewalls and antivirus software cannot stop an employee from voluntarily typing their password into a fake login page — that's why awareness training is a critical layer of defense.

### Common Types of Phishing

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Email phishing** | Mass emails impersonating a bank, vendor, or internal team |
| **Spear phishing** | Highly targeted emails using personal details about you or your company |
| **Whaling** | Spear phishing aimed at executives or high-value targets |
| **Smishing** | Phishing via SMS/text message |
| **Vishing** | Phishing via phone call or voicemail |
| **Business Email Compromise (BEC)** | Impersonating a colleague or vendor to redirect payments |
| **Clone phishing** | A copy of a legitimate email you already received, with links swapped for malicious ones |
| **Quishing** | Phishing using malicious QR codes |

## 2. How to Recognize Phishing Emails

Most phishing emails share recognizable warning signs. Train yourself to pause and check the following before clicking anything.

### 🚩 Red Flags Checklist

- [ ] Sender address doesn't match the organization (e.g., `support@paypa1-secure.com`)
- [ ] Generic greeting like "Dear Customer" instead of your name
- [ ] Urgency or fear-based language: *"Your account will be suspended in 24 hours."*
- [ ] Unexpected attachments, especially `.zip`, `.exe`, `.js`, or macro-enabled Office files
- [ ] Links where the display text doesn't match the actual destination URL
- [ ] Poor grammar, spelling mistakes, or inconsistent formatting/logos
- [ ] Requests for credentials, OTPs, gift cards, or wire transfers
- [ ] Slight misspellings of real domains (`paypa1.com`, `micros0ft.com`, `arnazon.com`)
- [ ] An offer that seems too good to be true (lottery wins, unexpected refunds)

> **💡 Quick Tip: Hover Before You Click**
> - On desktop, hover your mouse over a link (without clicking) to preview the real URL in the status bar.
> - On mobile, long-press a link to preview its destination.
> - If the domain doesn't exactly match the organization's known domain, don't click.

## 3. Recognizing Fake or Spoofed Websites

Attackers often clone real login pages (banking, email, SSO portals) to harvest credentials. These fake sites can look nearly identical to the real thing.

### How to Spot a Fake Website

- Check the URL carefully — look for extra words, hyphens, or a different domain extension (`.net` instead of `.com`)
- Look for HTTPS and a valid padlock icon — **but HTTPS alone does NOT guarantee legitimacy**; attackers use it too
- Verify the certificate details by clicking the padlock icon to see who issued it and to whom
- Be suspicious of pop-ups asking to "re-enter" your password or urgent security warnings
- Watch for low-quality images, broken layouts, or mismatched branding
- Never arrive at a login page by clicking an email link — type the known address directly or use a saved bookmark
- Use a password manager: it will not auto-fill credentials on a spoofed domain, which is a strong tell

## 4. Social Engineering Tactics Attackers Use

Phishing succeeds by exploiting human psychology rather than technical vulnerabilities. Recognizing these manipulation tactics helps you pause before reacting.

### Common Psychological Triggers

- **Authority** – posing as a CEO, IT admin, government agency, or law enforcement to demand compliance
- **Urgency & fear** – creating a false deadline or threat (account suspension, legal action, security breach)
- **Trust & familiarity** – impersonating a known colleague, vendor, or brand to lower your guard
- **Curiosity** – tempting subject lines like *"Your delivery couldn't be completed"* or *"See who viewed your profile"*
- **Reciprocity** – offering a fake gift, refund, or reward to prompt quick action
- **Scarcity** – *"Only 2 hours left to claim this offer"*, pushing hasty decisions
- **Pretexting** – attacker fabricates a believable scenario/story to justify an unusual request
- **Tailgating/piggybacking** (physical) – following an employee into a secure area without a badge

> **⚠️ Remember**
> - Legitimate organizations will never ask for your password, OTP, or full card number over email, text, or phone.
> - If a request feels rushed or emotionally charged, that's often intentional — slow down and verify independently.

## 5. Best Practices to Avoid Falling Victim

### For Every Employee

- Verify unexpected requests through a separate, known channel (call the person using a number you already have, not one in the email)
- Never click links or open attachments from unsolicited or unexpected emails
- Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) on all accounts
- Use a password manager and unique passwords per account
- Keep software, browsers, and antivirus tools updated
- Report suspicious emails using your organization's "Report Phishing" button instead of just deleting them
- When in doubt, don't click — ask your IT/security team first

### For Organizations

- Deploy email filtering, SPF/DKIM/DMARC, and anti-spoofing controls
- Run regular simulated phishing exercises and track click rates
- Maintain an easy one-click reporting mechanism and respond quickly to reports
- Provide ongoing training, not just a one-time session
- Establish verification procedures for wire transfers and vendor payment changes

## 6. Real-World Examples

### Case 1: Google & Facebook — $100M+ BEC Scam (2013–2015)
An attacker set up a fake company impersonating a legitimate hardware vendor both companies worked with, then sent convincing invoices over several years. Both companies wired over $100 million combined before the fraud was discovered.
> **Lesson:** Always verify banking-detail changes through a separate, trusted channel.

### Case 2: Twitter/X 2020 Bitcoin Scam
Attackers used vishing (phone-based social engineering) to trick Twitter employees into providing access to internal admin tools, then hijacked high-profile verified accounts to post a cryptocurrency scam.
> **Lesson:** Internal tools and employee trust are prime social-engineering targets, not just external customers.

### Case 3: Ubiquiti Networks Executive Impersonation (2015)
Employees in the finance department received emails impersonating a senior executive, requesting urgent wire transfers to an outside entity. The company lost approximately $46.7 million.
> **Lesson:** Urgency plus authority is a classic BEC combination — always confirm financial requests through a second channel.

### Case 4: COVID-19 Phishing Wave (2020–2021)
Attackers exploited public fear by sending emails impersonating health organizations, offering fake vaccine appointments, relief payments, or safety guidelines, leading to major spikes in credential theft.
> **Lesson:** Attackers rapidly weaponize current events and emotions — be extra cautious around major news cycles.

## 7. Knowledge Check: Phishing Awareness Quiz

*Test your understanding. Answers and explanations are provided directly below each question for self-paced learning.*

<details>
<summary><b>Q1. Which of the following is the BEST first step when you receive an unexpected email asking you to reset your password urgently?</b></summary>

A. Click the link immediately to avoid losing access
B. Reply to the email asking for more details
C. Verify the sender's address and hover over the link before doing anything
D. Forward it to a coworker to ask if it's real

**Answer: C**
*Explanation: Always inspect the sender address and the real destination of a link before interacting. Urgency is a common manipulation tactic.*
</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Q2. What does "vishing" refer to?</b></summary>

A. Phishing conducted through video calls only
B. Phishing conducted via voice calls or voicemail
C. A virus that spreads through phishing emails
D. Phishing that targets VIP executives

**Answer: B**
*Explanation: Vishing is voice phishing — social engineering carried out over phone calls or voicemail.*
</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Q3. Which URL is MOST likely to be a phishing attempt impersonating PayPal?</b></summary>

A. `https://www.paypal.com/signin`
B. `https://paypa1-secure-login.com`
C. `https://www.paypal.com/help`
D. `https://developer.paypal.com`

**Answer: B**
*Explanation: The domain uses a lookalike substitution ("paypa1" and an unrelated "-secure-login" domain) — a classic typosquatting technique.*
</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Q4. An email from your "CEO" urgently asks you to buy gift cards and send the codes immediately, asking you to keep it confidential. What should you do?</b></summary>

A. Buy the gift cards quickly since it's the CEO
B. Ignore it completely and take no action
C. Verify directly with the CEO through a known phone number or in person before acting
D. Reply to the email asking for their employee ID

**Answer: C**
*Explanation: This is a classic BEC/whaling scam. Urgency, authority, secrecy, and gift-card requests are major red flags — always verify out-of-band.*
</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Q5. Which of these is NOT a reliable sign that a website is safe?</b></summary>

A. The presence of HTTPS and a padlock icon
B. The exact, correctly spelled domain name
C. A valid certificate issued to the real organization
D. A professional-looking design and logo

**Answer: D**
*Explanation: Attackers can easily replicate professional designs and logos. HTTPS alone or good design doesn't guarantee legitimacy — always check domain and certificate details.*
</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Q6. What is "pretexting" in social engineering?</b></summary>

A. Sending a text message before a phone call
B. Creating a fabricated scenario to justify a request for information or access
C. Using pre-written phishing templates
D. A method for tracing the sender's true origin

**Answer: B**
*Explanation: Pretexting involves inventing a believable false context (e.g., posing as IT support) to manipulate the target into complying.*
</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Q7. You receive a text message claiming your package couldn't be delivered and asking you to click a link to reschedule. This is an example of:</b></summary>

A. Whaling
B. Smishing
C. Quishing
D. Clone phishing

**Answer: B**
*Explanation: Smishing is phishing delivered via SMS/text message, often using delivery or account alerts as bait.*
</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Q8. Which practice provides the STRONGEST additional protection even if your password is stolen?</b></summary>

A. Using a longer email signature
B. Enabling multi-factor authentication (MFA)
C. Changing your password color scheme in the manager
D. Turning off email notifications

**Answer: B**
*Explanation: MFA requires a second verification factor, so a stolen password alone is not enough for an attacker to gain access.*
</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Q9. What should you do if you suspect you already clicked a phishing link and entered your credentials?</b></summary>

A. Do nothing and hope for the best
B. Wait a week to see if anything happens
C. Immediately change your password, enable MFA, and report it to IT/security
D. Only tell a friend about it

**Answer: C**
*Explanation: Fast reporting and password changes limit the attacker's window to misuse compromised credentials.*
</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Q10. Which of the following BEST describes "whaling"?</b></summary>

A. Random mass phishing emails sent to thousands of people
B. Phishing attacks specifically targeting executives or high-value individuals
C. Phishing conducted exclusively through fake QR codes
D. A phishing attack that only targets banks

**Answer: B**
*Explanation: Whaling is a form of spear phishing that specifically targets senior executives or other high-value, high-authority individuals.*
</details>
